About

Climate change is a pressing global issue, characterized by rising temperatures, melting ice caps, and increasing extreme weather events, driven primarily by human activities such as deforestation and the burning of fossil fuels. Understanding its causes, impacts, and the urgent need for action is crucial to safeguarding our environment for future generations.

What is Climate Change?

 Climate change refers to significant changes in average weather conditions—such as becoming warmer, wetter, or drier—over several decades or more. This long-term trend distinguishes climate change from natural weather variability. Its effects are diverse, including record floods, raging storms, and deadly heat, impacting all living beings, but not equally. Economically disadvantaged communities and people of color, who contribute the least to its causes, suffer the most from its impacts. The basics include understanding the causes of climate change, its effects on the planet and people, and potential solutions.

What is Climate Change and Global Heating?
How Does it Work to Affect Our Lives?

 Climate change, or global warming, results from Earth's average temperature increase due to rising greenhouse gas concentrations and human-induced emissions. Greenhouse gases such as water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone trap heat radiation in the atmosphere, intensifying warming by absorbing and emitting heat. This phenomenon alters global climate patterns, driven primarily by human activities, with scientific consensus attributing over 95% of observed warming to these factors.

The climate crisis: the causes, the effects and the solutions

 Climate change has always existed, but the past 150 years of global warming are unusual due to human activity, known as the anthropogenic greenhouse effect. This began with the industrial revolution, leading to a dramatic rise in carbon dioxide levels, now at 410-415 ppm compared to the historical 200-180 ppm. Monitored by observatories like Mauna Loa, data analyzed by the IPCC over the past 15 years confirms that global warming is due to human activity, a link first established by Svante Arrhenius and later confirmed by David Keeling in the 1960s.

The Arctic and the Philippines: An Unexpected Connection

 As the Arctic warms, it impacts global weather patterns and sea levels, directly affecting island nations like the Philippines. The accelerated melting of the Greenland Ice Sheet is a major contributor to rising sea levels, which, combined with cyclones, floods, and droughts, poses serious threats to coastal communities in the Philippines. Annual temperature and rainfall variations in the country are notably influenced by El Niño and La Niña phenomena. The melting Arctic sea ice is expected to contribute to more frequent and intense El Niño events, exacerbating temperature rises and leading to issues like water scarcity and drought.

Plastic Ban And Regulation

Indulge The Cabinet Cluster on Climate Change Adaptation approved Resolution No. 1 to phase out single-use plastics and promote sustainable products. This includes the Philippine Action Plan for Sustainable Consumption, led by NEDA, and establishes a group to support an Anti-Single Use Plastics Law. Additionally, the Climate Change Commission has banned plastics in its office, and the House Committee on Ecology approved House Bill 9147 to regulate single-use plastics.

Communities For Resilience

Efforts to defeat poverty and promote social justice require helping vulnerable communities adapt to climate change. Studies show climate changes will impact agriculture, water resources, health, and urban areas, affecting food and water security and environmental stability. In response, the Climate Change Commission implemented the CORE Capacity Building Training Program, aligning with national and global frameworks.

Gawad CCC: Search for Outstanding Young Climate Scientists

The Gawad CCC is a contest for high school students to present research on climate change issues. In 2019, it included four regional recognition activities and a climate science bootcamp to enhance their research before the National Gawad CCC. Coordinated with the Department of Education, the initiative resulted in four projects aimed at developing climate change adaptation and mitigation strategies.

Anti Single Use Plastic Campaign

Single-use plastics (SUPs) represent a throwaway culture that harms the environment and human health. They pollute water bodies, harm marine life, clog drainage systems, and release toxic gases during production, transportation, and disposal. Plastics have long lifetimes, with bottles lasting around 450 years. Instead of biodegrading, they break down into microplastics, which are ingested by fish and enter the human food chain.

Redraw The Line

Between 2010 and 2014, The Media Alliance managed the "Redraw the Line" campaign to raise public awareness of climate change in the Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam, and later Bangladesh. Supported by Sida and ADB, the campaign aimed to raise awareness of climate change factors, encourage demand for environmentally responsible products, and promote behavior changes in consumption practices.

Filipino youth mobilise for climate justice

YACAP is an alliance of Filipino youth organizations, student councils, and individuals fighting against climate change and pursuing climate justice. Established in 2019 as the counterpart of Fridays for Future in the Philippines, YACAP has chapters nationwide and aims to expand further. They engage youth and frontline communities in climate education, advocate for alternatives to problematic government policies, and collaborate with global activists to drive systemic change. YACAP organizes activities like social media campaigns, strikes, education programs, and a podcast on the climate crisis. Recent initiatives include the Youth Climate Agenda 2022, promoting green leadership in national elections, and the Global Climate Strike in March 2022.

Resilience And Preparedness Towards Inclusive Development

The RAPID Program, an expansion of Project Climate Twin Phoenix, supported long-term recovery for LGUs and communities in Yolanda-affected areas along San Pedro Bay and Leyte Gulf. It focused on risk management, including risk assessments, emergency preparedness, disaster risk reduction, and improved regulatory frameworks. Running from 2014 to 2019, it was funded by the Australian Government and implemented by UNDP with CCC as the lead agency. The program's culminating activity was on July 12, 2019.

SCIENTIFIC RESEARCH

Oil and Gas Assets at Risk

 The study indicates that even with current and moderate global climate policies, the international oil and gas sector will undergo significant structural changes over the next two decades. This shift poses challenges for U.S. regions heavily dependent on these industries, which may struggle to remain economically competitive. Global oil demand is expected to decline, particularly if electric vehicles gain market share as anticipated. Similarly, U.S. gas production faces challenges despite stable global demand. The study emphasizes the need for federal policies to support economic diversification and workforce reorganization in affected communities, ensuring they can adapt during the transition to cleaner energy sources.

POLICY UPDATES

The Politics of Climate Change

 Environmental groups are grappling with their response to the Waxman-Markey climate bill, officially known as H.R. 2454, the American Clean Energy and Security Act of 2009 (ACES). This comprehensive legislation, nearly 1,000 pages long, is moving through the House of Representatives with a coalition that seems sufficient for passage, although challenges are anticipated in the Senate. Compromises made to garner support in the House have been difficult for many environmental organizations to accept. Some have outright opposed the bill, others demand it be strengthened before they will support it, and some have taken a neutral stance. Meanwhile, business-oriented environmental groups like NRDC and EDF have voiced strong support for the bill.

POLICY UPDATES

Good for the Planet and our Pockets: The Time tO Invest in Low-Carbon Technology is now

 Improving the energy efficiency of homes in the UK is critical, as they lose heat much faster than those in many European countries, largely due to widespread use of gas boilers. Currently, homes account for 15 to 20 percent of the nation's carbon emissions, highlighting the urgency of decarbonizing properties to meet legally binding net zero targets. Transitioning to low-carbon homes not only reduces emissions but also decreases reliance on fossil fuels, enhancing energy affordability and security while generating thousands of jobs nationwide. A report by WWF and ScottishPower, "Better Homes, Cooler Planet," underscores the additional benefits: installing solar panels, heat pumps, batteries, and electric vehicle charging points, alongside energy efficiency upgrades, can increase property values by an average of £10,000 across England and Wales, with some homes experiencing even greater appreciation.

SUCCESS STORIES

A Breath of Freash Air

 Tanzanias Pugu, Vikindu, and Kazimzumbwi coastal forests represent some of the world's oldest surviving forest remnants, now encompassed within the Pugu-Kazimzumbwi Nature Forest Reserve. These areas are rich in biodiversity and serve as crucial resources for nearby communities, earning the nickname 'the lungs of Dar es Salaam'. However, rapid urban expansion in Dar es Salaam has intensified threats such as deforestation, fires, and unsustainable agriculture, endangering these ancient forests. WWF-Tanzania, in collaboration with Trillion Trees, the Tanzania Forest Service (TFS), and district councils, is actively engaged in forest restoration efforts. Initiatives include establishing the Pugu-Kazimzumbwi Nature Reserve for enhanced protection and planting 24,500 native tree seedlings in 2021 alone, totaling 73,500 seedlings over five years. Initial assessments show signs of natural regeneration, although ongoing monitoring and further restoration efforts are necessary to ensure the forests' recovery and sustainability.

NEWS AND UPDATES IN CATBALOGAN

Forest-Related Greenhouse Gas Fluxes In Catbalogan City, Samar Philippines

 In 2010, Catbalogan City had 7.00 square kilometers of natural forest, covering 62% of its land area. By 2023, it had lost 21 hectares of natural forest, resulting in emissions equivalent to 25.2 kilotons of CO₂. Between 2001 and 2023, the city's forests emitted an average of 24.6 kilotons of CO₂e per year, while removing -84.4 kilotons of CO₂e per year, indicating a net carbon sink of -59.8 kilotons of CO₂e per year.
